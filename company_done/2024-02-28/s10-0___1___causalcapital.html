About us

Causal Capital Pte Ltd is a Singaporean risk consultancy specializing in structured finance. We work with banks, hedge funds, brokerages, energy companies, utilities, construction and big pharma to deliver solutions for risk management. Our focus is on Investment Strategies, Enterprise Risk Management, Market Risk and Counterparty Credit Risk. All aspects of a risk framework can be managed by our team taking in: audit, training, policy writing, data capture, risk system design / development and risk modelling. Causal Capital has several partnerships with vendors offering cutting edge credit, treasury and trading solutions.

    The spread of unverified facts and figures can lead not only to widespread misconceptions but also to strategic missteps. Dr. Robert Joslin, founder of the AIPMO - Association of International Project Management Officers (AIPMO), encountered such a scenario, which serves as a poignant reminder of the importance of diligent research and skepticism towards commonly accepted "truths." In 2017, Dr. Joslin was preparing to deliver a keynote in London, centered around the alarming statistic that 50% PMOs fail within two years. This figure, however, upon closer inspection, turned out to be a misinterpretation. The original research conducted by Monique Aubry in 2010 was misread by the Gartner Group, leading to the propagation of this misleading statistic. Aubry's study, focusing on 18 PMOs across 15 companies in Sweden and Canada, did not conclude that these PMOs failed; rather, it observed that organizations undergo changes every two years, within which PMOs evolved — not failed. Upon discovering this discrepancy, Dr. Joslin reached out to Monique Aubry, who confirmed the misunderstanding and later published a paper to clarify this false paradigm. Despite this clarification, the Project Management Institute had already disseminated the incorrect interpretation, embedding the erroneous statistic within the professional community's consciousness. Dr. Joslin had to not only adjust his presentation but also to emphasize the critical lesson of verifying the source and accuracy of information before accepting it as fact. This incident highlights the peril of generalizing data from a small sample to a global context, a common research oversight that can lead to the establishment of illusory truths. The phenomenon of "illusory truth", where repeated statements are perceived as more truthful regardless of their veracity, underscores the necessity for critical thinking and rigorous scrutiny in the face of widely accepted beliefs. The adherence to such myths is not unique to project management but is a historical human oversight, as seen in practices like bloodletting as a cure for illness, or the use of radium in everyday products during the 1940s, leading to tragic outcomes. Dr. Joslin's experience serves as an important reminder: In a world brimming with information, the onus is on each professional to discern fact from fiction, ensuring that decisions and strategies are grounded in verified truths rather than convenient assumptions. This approach not only safeguards the integrity of the project management profession but also fosters a culture of informed skepticism and continuous learning.
